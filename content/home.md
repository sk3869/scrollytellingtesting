## A deep dive into the science

# Alzheimer's Disease

*The world's most prevalent neurodegenerative condition — and the causes behind it.*

Affecting **55 million people** worldwide, Alzheimer's is a progressive disorder that destroys memory, thinking, and the ability to carry out the simplest tasks. Understanding its causes is the first step toward prevention and treatment.

## Overview

# What Is Alzheimer's?

Alzheimer's disease accounts for **60–80%** of all dementia cases. It is not a normal part of aging — it is a disease that gradually destroys neurons and the connections between them, beginning deep in the brain years before any symptoms appear.

```stat-grid
55M | People living with dementia worldwide
10M | New cases diagnosed every year
3rd | Leading cause of death in older adults (U.S.)
2050 | When cases are projected to nearly triple
```

*Sources: WHO 2023, Alzheimer's Association Facts & Figures 2024*

## Cause 01 — Biological

# Amyloid Plaques

Beta-amyloid is a protein fragment that normally gets cleared from the brain. In Alzheimer's, it misfolds and clumps into sticky plaques between neurons — disrupting cell-to-cell signaling and triggering immune responses that damage surrounding tissue.

- Plaques begin forming **15–20 years before** any symptoms appear
- They block synaptic transmission, directly impairing memory formation
- Amyloid-β42 — the longer, stickier variant — is the most toxic form
- PET brain scans can now detect plaques in living patients, enabling earlier diagnosis

> "Amyloid plaques are the hallmark pathology of Alzheimer's — but their presence alone does not guarantee cognitive decline. Context matters."

---

## Cause 02 — Biological

# Neurofibrillary Tangles

Tau is a protein that normally stabilizes the internal skeleton of neurons. In Alzheimer's, tau becomes hyperphosphorylated — chemically altered — and collapses into twisted fibers called neurofibrillary tangles that clog and kill neurons from the inside.

- Tangles spread through the brain in a predictable pattern, beginning in the hippocampus
- The **Braak staging system** (I–VI) maps tangle progression to symptom severity
- Tau tangles disrupt nutrient and energy transport inside the neuron, starving it
- New tau-targeting therapies are in active Phase III clinical trials as of 2024

> "Tau tangles follow amyloid plaques like a second wave — amplifying neuronal death across the entire cortex."

## Cause 03 — Genetic

# Genetics & the APOE-ε4 Gene

The APOE gene comes in three variants: ε2, ε3, and ε4. Inheriting one copy of APOE-ε4 roughly **triples** your lifetime risk of Alzheimer's. Two copies raises that risk by up to **12×**. APOE-ε4 impairs the brain's ability to clear amyloid and accelerates plaque formation.

- ~25% of people carry one APOE-ε4 allele; ~2–3% carry two copies
- **Early-onset Alzheimer's** (before age 65) is often linked to rare mutations in APP, PSEN1, or PSEN2
- Individuals with Down syndrome carry an extra APP gene, making dementia near-universal by age 60
- Genetic risk is not destiny — lifestyle factors significantly modulate expression

## Cause 04 — Risk Factor

# Age — The Primary Risk Factor

Age is the single greatest known risk factor for Alzheimer's. The risk roughly **doubles every five years** after age 65. By age 85, nearly one in three people will have the disease. Aging impairs the brain's natural waste-clearance systems, allowing amyloid and tau to accumulate unchecked.

```stat-grid
5% | of people aged 65–74 are living with Alzheimer's
13% | of people aged 75–84 are living with Alzheimer's
33% | of people aged 85+ are living with Alzheimer's
73% | of all Alzheimer's patients are over 75 years old
```

## Cause 05 — Vascular

# Cardiovascular Risk Factors

The brain consumes 20% of the body's entire blood supply. Anything that restricts or damages that supply — high blood pressure, heart disease, diabetes, high cholesterol, or stroke — accelerates neuronal death and dramatically increases Alzheimer's risk.

- **Hypertension in midlife** is one of the most modifiable risk factors, accounting for ~8% of cases
- Type 2 diabetes doubles dementia risk — insulin resistance directly impairs brain energy metabolism
- Stroke doubles Alzheimer's risk independently of all other factors
- Atherosclerosis reduces blood flow to the brain and triggers chronic neuroinflammation

> "What is good for the heart is good for the brain."

## Cause 06 — Physical

# Head Trauma & TBI

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) — from falls, sports injuries, or accidents — significantly elevates long-term dementia risk. Severe TBIs trigger immediate amyloid-β accumulation and chronic tau pathology, essentially compressing decades of biological aging into a single event.

- A single moderate-to-severe TBI increases lifetime Alzheimer's risk by **2–4×**
- Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE), linked to repeated concussions in athletes, shares core pathology with Alzheimer's
- Falls are the #1 cause of TBI in adults over 65 — the same population most at risk for dementia
- The lag between TBI and dementia onset is typically **10–30 years**

---

## Cause 07 — Immune

# Neuroinflammation

The brain's immune cells — microglia — normally clear debris and protect neurons. In Alzheimer's, their chronic activation creates a persistent inflammatory environment that amplifies amyloid and tau damage rather than resolving it.

- Variants in the **TREM2 gene** — a microglial receptor — are among the strongest genetic risk factors outside APOE-ε4
- Chronic systemic inflammation from infections, obesity, or autoimmune conditions can cross the blood-brain barrier
- Elevated inflammatory markers like IL-6 and TNF-α are found in patients **years before** diagnosis
- Anti-inflammatory diets (Mediterranean, MIND) show strong protective correlations in large cohort studies

> "Neuroinflammation is not just a side effect — it is a co-driver of neurodegeneration."

---

## Cause 08 — Lifestyle

# Sleep Disorders

During deep sleep, the brain's glymphatic system flushes out metabolic waste — including amyloid-β. Chronic sleep deprivation or disorders like sleep apnea allow amyloid to accumulate faster than it can be cleared, accelerating plaque formation by years.

- Just **one night** of sleep deprivation causes measurable amyloid-β increases in cerebrospinal fluid
- People with untreated sleep apnea develop Alzheimer's pathology **10 years earlier** on average
- Glymphatic clearance is 60% more active during sleep than wakefulness
- Treating sleep apnea and improving sleep hygiene may significantly reduce long-term risk

---

## Cause 09 — Modifiable

# Lifestyle Factors

Up to **40% of Alzheimer's cases** worldwide may be attributable to modifiable lifestyle and environmental risk factors, according to the landmark 2020 Lancet Commission on Dementia Prevention. This is among the most hopeful findings in Alzheimer's research.

- **Physical inactivity** — sedentary behavior reduces BDNF and causes hippocampal shrinkage
- **Obesity** — midlife obesity raises risk by ~60%; adipose tissue produces pro-inflammatory cytokines
- **Smoking** — doubles Alzheimer's risk; causes oxidative stress and cerebrovascular damage
- **Excessive alcohol** — heavy use causes direct neurotoxicity and reduces overall brain volume
- **Low education** — reduced cognitive reserve leaves less neurological buffer against the disease
- **Social isolation** — chronic loneliness doubles dementia risk, likely through sustained stress and inflammation

---

## Cause 10 — Environmental

# Environmental Toxins

Growing evidence links chronic exposure to environmental toxins with accelerated neurodegeneration. Air pollution, heavy metals, and pesticides can cross the blood-brain barrier, trigger inflammation, and mimic or amplify the molecular changes seen in Alzheimer's.

- **Air pollution (PM2.5)** — associated with a **40% increase** in dementia risk in long-term exposure studies
- **Heavy metals** — aluminum, lead, and mercury accumulate in brain tissue and promote tau and amyloid aggregation
- **Pesticides** — organophosphates and DDT metabolites are found at higher concentrations in Alzheimer's patients
- **Chronic stress (cortisol)** — sustained elevated cortisol shrinks the hippocampus and impairs memory consolidation

> "The brain is not isolated from its environment. What we breathe, eat, and absorb over a lifetime shapes our neurological future."

## The path forward

# Knowledge Is the First Step

Alzheimer's does not have a single cause — it is a convergence of biology, genetics, lifestyle, and environment. But every cause identified opens a door: to prevention, to early detection, to treatment.

*Every modifiable risk factor is a potential target for intervention. The research continues — and so does the hope.*
